My Personal Journey with HiveMind

Alessio Ikenna Akabuogu  |  Project Lead, Group 3 — Christoph Dashboard
HiveMind Academy × Ravensbourne University  |  2025–2026

1. Where It All Began

When I found out I was going to be Project Lead for Group 3, I felt two things at the same time — excited and a bit nervous. I had never led a team on a real industry project before. This was not a made-up university assignment. HiveMind Academy is a real company, with real problems, and they were counting on us to help them figure out whether their product ideas actually worked.

Our job was simple to explain but hard to do: find out if the HiveMind dashboard would genuinely help people at work. The person we were designing for was called "Christoph" — a fictional but very realistic new employee who is totally overwhelmed on his first few weeks at a job. He has information scattered everywhere, no idea where to look, and too many tools open at once. Sound familiar? It did to me.

My team was made up of five people: Sish (our researcher), Johannu (our IA designer), Md Sium (our UI designer), and Adnan (our tech lead). Everyone had their role. My job was to hold it all together.

2. Week One — Listening First

Before we touched Figma or wrote a single line of code, we talked to real people. Sish led six interviews with working professionals — people in banking, engineering, IT, tech, and sales. I read every single transcript. That was my choice as Project Lead, because I did not want to make decisions based on summaries someone else wrote.

What I read surprised me. Every single person, no matter what job they had, said the same kind of thing. Information was all over the place. Finding a document took ages. Nobody trusted AI tools because they did not know where the answers came from. One person — a junior network engineer — said this:

"One dashboard shows the region as healthy, another shows severe degradation. Who do you trust? No one knows. You just cross-check manually."

That quote stuck with me. It made everything feel real. These were not just design challenges — they were things causing people actual stress every day. By the end of Week 1, I had helped Sish write five hypotheses. These were basically educated guesses we wanted to test with our design. One of them was:

"We believe that showing the source document next to every AI answer will help users trust the AI more, because right now nobody knows where the answers come from."

That became one of the most important ideas in our whole project.

3. Week Two — Making Hard Choices

Week 2 was when things got real in a different way. Johannu had started mapping out five different screens we wanted to design. Md Sium was excited about the onboarding flow. But when I looked at what we had left — two more weeks, including testing — I knew we could not do all five properly.

I had to tell the team we were cutting two screens. That was uncomfortable. Nobody wants to hear that their work is being shelved. But I explained it simply: three great screens, properly tested, will help HiveMind far more than five average screens with no real findings. The team understood. We documented the decision and moved on.

The three flows we kept were: the main dashboard, the Knowledge Hub search, and the AI assistant. Johannu drew the information architecture — basically a map of how all the pages connect — and Md Sium started sketching wireframes. Simple grey boxes, no colour, just structure. It worked.

4. Week Three — Design and Code Come Together

This was my favourite week. Md Sium turned the wireframes into proper high-fidelity designs in Figma, and Adnan started building real React components using the IBM Carbon Design System. I was working across both — reviewing designs in the afternoon, reviewing code at night.

The design I cared most about was the AI assistant screen. Based on what we heard in the interviews, every AI response needed to show users exactly where the answer came from. Here is what the final layout looked like:

┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ [AI icon] AI Assistant [HIGH CONFIDENCE ✓] │ │────────────────────────────────────────────────────────│ │ Your question: │ │ "What is the expense limit for client meals?" │ │ │ │ Answer: │ │ The limit is £50 per person, subject to manager │ │ approval for anything over £30. │ │ │ │ ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ │ │ 📄 Finance & Expenses Policy v2.3 │ │ │ │ Last updated: 14 Feb 2026 │ │ │ │ [Open full document →] │ │ │ └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ │ │ │ │ Was this helpful? [👍 Yes] [👎 Ask a person] │ └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

Figure 1 — AI Assistant response screen layout. Every answer shows the source document, version number, and last updated date.

The source block — the bit showing the document name, version, and date — was my idea based directly on the interviews. Every person who did not trust AI said the same thing: "I don't know where it gets the answer from." So we made it impossible to miss.

On the code side, Adnan built two Carbon components. I specified what they needed to do. The first was the AI Dashboard Tile. It shows an AI answer on the main dashboard with a colour-coded confidence level — green for high, grey for medium, red for low. Here is the logic for that badge:

const confidenceConfig = {
  high:   { type: 'green', icon: <Checkmark size={12} />,
            label: 'High confidence' },
  medium: { type: 'warm-gray', icon: null,
            label: 'Medium confidence' },
  low:    { type: 'red', icon: <Warning size={12} />,
            label: 'Low confidence — verify with your manager' },
};

const conf = confidenceConfig[confidence] ?? confidenceConfig.medium;

<Tag type={conf.type} size="sm">
  {conf.icon}
  {conf.label}
</Tag>

Code 1 — Confidence badge logic in AIDashboardTile.jsx. Uses colour AND text so it works for colour-blind users too.

This matters because the badge uses both colour and words — not just colour alone. That means it is accessible to people who are colour-blind, which is something I pushed for when reviewing Adnan's work.

The second component was the AI Assistant Input. One thing that came up in the interviews was privacy — people were worried about typing sensitive things into an AI tool. So I asked Adnan to add a warning that appears automatically when someone types words like "salary," "promotion," or "disciplinary." It looks like this:

const isSensitiveQuery = [
  'salary', 'pay', 'performance review',
  'promotion', 'disciplinary'
].some((term) => question.toLowerCase().includes(term));

{isSensitiveQuery && (
  <InlineNotification
    kind="warning"
    title="Sensitive topic detected"
    subtitle="This may need a human response. Consider
    speaking with HR or your manager directly."
  />
)}

Code 2 — Sensitive topic detection in AIAssistantInput.jsx. Triggers a warning banner if the user's question touches HR or personal topics.

It is a small detail, but it shows users that the system is aware of its own limits. Ironically, that makes people trust it more, not less.

At the end of Week 3, we did a dry run. I played Christoph and clicked through the whole prototype while the team watched and took notes. We found three dead ends and two labels that were confusing. We fixed all of them before testing week.

5. Week Four — Did It Actually Work?

Week 4 was testing week. Sish ran five sessions with real working professionals, and I sat in the background writing observation notes. Watching someone use something you helped build is a strange feeling — a mix of hope and nervousness.

The results were better than I expected. Our System Usability Scale (SUS) score — basically a standard usability score out of 100 — came back at 82 out of 100. Anything above 80 is considered "Good." We had aimed for 80, so we hit our target.

The AI source transparency feature performed the best out of everything we built. All five participants found the source document within 30 seconds without being asked to look for it. Four of them said they would actually act on the AI answer without checking elsewhere. One participant said:

"Oh, it shows me where it comes from — that's actually really useful."

That comment meant more to me than the SUS score. It meant the core idea — the one that came from reading the transcripts in Week 1 — had worked.

Not everything was perfect. One label called "Directory" confused a participant who was looking for team contacts. We documented it as a recommendation: rename it to "Team Contacts." Small change, big difference.

6. What I Learned

Being Project Lead taught me things I could not have learned from a textbook. The biggest one was this: cutting scope is not failure. In Week 2, I reduced our five planned screens to three. At the time it felt like giving up. By Week 4, it was clearly the right call — the three flows we kept were polished, properly tested, and backed by real evidence.

I also learned that the best ideas came from the research, not from us. The source attribution on the AI screen, the sensitive topic warning, the confidence badges — all of these came from things real people told us in interviews. Our job as designers and developers was to translate those words into working features.

Leading a team also meant having uncomfortable conversations. Telling Johannu that two of his sketched flows were cut. Asking Adnan to rebuild a component because the carbon token references were wrong. Pushing Md Sium to fix three broken prototype links the day before testing. None of that is glamorous. But it is what a lead does.

The thing I am most proud of from this whole project is not the SUS score or the Figma prototype. It is the fact that HiveMind told us our findings would genuinely shape their product roadmap. That made four weeks of work feel like it counted for something real.

7. Final Thought

If I had to describe this experience in one sentence, it would be: I came in thinking design was about making things look good, and I left knowing it is about making things make sense.

HiveMind gave our group a real problem with real stakes. We gave them real answers. That is the best kind of university project there is.
